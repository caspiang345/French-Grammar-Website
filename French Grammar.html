French Grammar Rules for -er, -re, and -ir Verbs

What is verb conjugation?

Verb conjugation is when you take a verb in the infinitive form and change it to the proper form for the subject. In most languages, there are 6 different subjects: singular and plural 1st, 2nd, and 3rd person. Without proper knoledge of the subject, you'll be as lost as a moneky in space when trying to speak French, so let's get into it. Below is a table of the 6 subjects in English.

Why is verb conjugation important?

Verb conjugation allowx you to transform words from simply abstract things into a form that is applicable in a sentence. While far less common than in French, English conjugates verbs too! We're going to use to infinitive verb to be/etre to demonstrate this concept.

Examples of English verb conjugation

While four of the six subjects use the same form of to be, that being are, there is still a distinction for some of the subjects. As we will see below, this is not the case with French.

As opposed to English, the French have a different form of etre for every subject. This is why verb conjugation is important for learning French; while it is something that us English speakers have hardly any need for, it is a necessity for the French.

Present tense conjugation for the three main verb types

To start off with, there are three main verb types which accompany the vast majority of French verbs. Those verbs are those that end in -er, -re, and -ir. We will look at their present tense forms below.

-Er verbs

-Er verbs make up 90% of the total number of verbs in French. Therefore, learning how to conjugate this set of verbs is crucial for understanding a huge part of French. It's important to remember that even though these words end in -er in the infinitive, they are pronounced not as though they have an 'e' and an 'r', but simply an 'e' pronounced like "aye". Example of -er verbs include:

Manger
To eat
Nager
To swim
Changer
To change
Parler
To speak
Acheter
To buy

-Re verbs

This group of verbs is pronounced in the infinitive with an ending that sounds like the first part of the word rou-gh (ex. com-prawnd-ruh). Examples of -re verbs include:

Rendre
To give back
Prendre
To take
Attendre
To wait
Vendre
To sell
Apprendre
To learn

Irregular Verbs

Despite the promenince of these 3 structures for verb conjugation, there are some verbs that do not follow the rules. These verbs are called irregular verbs. What you might not expect is that some of these irregular verbs are both some of the most commonly used verbs and a lot end in either -er, -re, or -ir with just a slightly differnet ending for a couple of the subjects. Examples of irregular verbs include:

Aller
To go
Avoir
To have
Dire
To say
Faire
To do
Pouvoir
To be able to
